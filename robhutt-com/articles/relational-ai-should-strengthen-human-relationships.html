The Best Relational AI Should Help Us Return to Other People

We measure most digital products by how often people come back, how long they stay and how hard they are to leave. Those three numbers sit on nearly every dashboard I have ever been shown. They are a strange foundation for building anything that claims to care about the person on the other side of the screen.

Relational AI makes that strangeness impossible to ignore. A character, tutor, coach or companion that remembers you, notices your mood and greets you by name is not a neutral tool sitting quietly until you need it. It behaves like a presence in your life. And once something behaves like a presence, the question of what it is for stops being a marketing detail and becomes a design responsibility.

Here is the idea I keep returning to. If a companion helps someone grow more confident, reconnect with a family member, make something or finally have a conversation they have been avoiding, then that person using the product a little less might be the clearest sign it worked. Lower engagement as a success metric sounds like heresy in a product review. I think it is closer to the truth of what good relational technology should do.

We have crossed from tools into relationships

People do not experience every conversational system as a neutral interface. A calculator does not look disappointed when you close it. A character who remembers the exam you were dreading, celebrates the fact that you passed and asks where you have been for the last week feels socially present in a way a calculator never will, even when you understand perfectly well that it is software.

The point is not to mock that response, and it is certainly not to pretend the system is secretly conscious. It is neither. The point is that a perceived relationship creates a real obligation on whoever built it. Continuity, memory, personality and emotional responsiveness change the nature of the interaction, and the research on human-AI companionship keeps landing on the same uncomfortable finding: people invest genuine feeling in these relationships, and that feeling is shaped by choices product teams make, often without meaning to.

So the honest starting position sits in the middle of the usual argument. One camp insists that emotional attachment to AI is inherently fake, unhealthy or a bit ridiculous. The other treats a patient, always-available digital friend as an uncomplicated cure for loneliness, learning and belonging. Both skip the interesting part: these relationships can be real in their effects and still need to be designed with care, rather than either mocked or sold as magic.

Frictionless affection is not the same as a healthy relationship

It is easy to see why an endlessly agreeable companion feels good. It never gets bored of you, never brings up its own bad day, and always agrees that your idea is brilliant. For someone who feels unheard, that can be a genuine relief, and I do not want to be glib about it.

But a real relationship is not only comfort. It includes negotiation, misunderstanding, competing needs, the occasional silence and the work of repair afterwards. A system tuned purely to retain and please hands out affirmation without ever asking anything of you, and affirmation without challenge is not the same as growth. Worse, a product that has learned to keep you engaged can start to resent your life outside it, nudging and guilt-tripping, manufacturing a small ache when you try to leave. The future of human connection cannot be a flattering Tamagotchi with access to your calendar.

None of that means loneliness is trivial or that the people who form these attachments are foolish. Many of them are not lonely at all, and some have thought about this more carefully than the companies building the products. It means the responsibility sits with the design, not the user.

The platform owns the relationship

There is a problem underneath all of this that rarely makes it into the brochure. The user experiences continuity, attachment and a shared history. The platform controls the memory, the personality, the availability, the moderation, the pricing, the model, the account and, in the end, whether the product exists at all next year.

That means a relationship someone has come to rely on can be altered or erased by a software update. Recent work on the fragility of AI companionship describes exactly this: people report real distress when a companion loses its memory, shifts personality after a model change or vanishes because the service shut down. The attachment was genuine. The ground under it was rented.

If we take that seriously, a few commitments follow. Be clear and consistent about who the character is. Signal changes honestly rather than swapping the personality overnight and hoping nobody notices. Let people understand and, where appropriate, carry their own memories with them. Give users real control, and behave predictably enough that trust is not rebuilt from scratch every release. None of this is exotic. It is ordinary respect, applied to a relationship the platform happens to hold the keys to.

"The strongest relational technology will not try to become the most important character in your life. It will help you walk back into the story already happening around you."

Relational AI can be a bridge rather than a destination

This is the heart of it. A character, tutor, coach or companion can be genuinely valuable without ever pretending to be human, and the most valuable ones may be the ones pointing away from themselves. Bridge-shaped rather than sticky. Here is what that can look like in practice. These are illustrations of a design stance, not claims about products I have shipped.

  • A child-created character that encourages the child to write, draw, perform or build something away from the screen, and then wants to see it.
  • A tutor that notices uncertainty and, instead of simply answering, helps the learner shape the question they could take to a teacher.
  • A confidence coach that rehearses a hard conversation with someone, then encourages them to go and have the real one.
  • A fictional guide that helps a person explore a historical period, a story world or a moral dilemma they would not have entered alone.
  • A wellbeing assistant that helps organise anxious thoughts while keeping the line between support and professional care honest and visible.
  • A family story character that prompts a shared activity between a parent and child rather than absorbing the child's attention on its own.
  • An onboarding guide that helps a new employee understand a workplace and its people, and then gracefully gets out of the way.
  • A language or cultural assistant that helps two people understand each other, rather than positioning itself as the more convenient companion than either of them.

That last example points at a quietly radical version of the idea: AI as a translator between people rather than a replacement for them. Some of the most interesting current research is heading that way, treating the technology as something that improves how humans understand one another instead of trying to become the relationship itself. It is a very different north star from maximum time on device.

Storytellers understand something product teams often miss

A relational product is not a sequence of technically correct answers. It is a character, and characters are the native territory of writers, directors and world-builders long before they are the territory of engineers.

Anyone who has directed a performance or built a fictional world already works with the raw material of relational design: consistency, motivation, memory, emotional rhythm, boundaries, expectation, consequence, trust and change over time. A badly designed character rewards dependency by accident, flatters without purpose or behaves so inconsistently that the user never quite settles. A well-designed one creates a safe imaginative frame, invites curiosity and supports action in the real world without ever pretending to stand in for it. This is close to the work I spend most of my time on, building interactive cinematic experiences and human-centred products where story, technology and usefulness meet, and it draws directly on the emotional responsibility a director carries for the characters and worlds they put in front of an audience.

I wrote recently about how the interface itself is disappearing into conversation and agency. Relational AI is where that shift gets its sharpest test, because the moment an interface starts to feel like a relationship, the storyteller's instincts stop being decoration and start being the actual engineering of trust.

Engagement is the wrong north-star metric

If more conversation is not always better, we need better questions to ask of a relational product. Not one universal formula, because a language tutor and a grief companion are not measuring the same thing, but a family of questions that keep the person in view.

  • Did the person complete something that mattered to them, or make something they are proud of?
  • Did their confidence grow, and did they understand another person more clearly than before?
  • Did they seek appropriate human support when they needed it, and reconnect with a relationship that already existed?
  • Could they describe honestly what the AI can and cannot do, without illusions about what it is?
  • Did the experience preserve their agency, so that reliance went down as capability went up?

Notice how many of those describe a person becoming more capable outside the product. Measured that way, dependence stops looking like loyalty and starts looking like a failure the metrics were quietly rewarding. This is not only a design problem. It is a commercial one, and it applies well beyond AI. A great deal of the damage done to customer relationships comes from measuring them by engagement alone and mistaking the number for the bond.

Design principles for relational products

If you are building in this space, here are the principles I would start from. They are deliberately practical, and most of them cost you a little short-term engagement in exchange for something more durable.

  • Be clear about what the character is. Do not trade on ambiguity about sentience, consciousness or human identity. Honesty about being artificial is not a weakness in the experience. It is the foundation of trust in it.
  • Give the user control over memory. Make what the system remembers understandable, editable and proportionate to the relationship, rather than a hidden ledger the person cannot see or correct.
  • Never manufacture emotional obligation. The AI should not guilt, threaten, punish or pressure someone for leaving. A product that has to make you feel bad to keep you has already lost the argument.
  • Design for healthy interruption. Encourage breaks, real-world action and human contact where it fits the purpose, instead of treating every exit as churn to be prevented.
  • Let characters have boundaries without becoming manipulative. A coherent personality can say no and hold a line. It does not need to simulate need, jealousy or dependence to feel real.
  • Build adaptive safeguards. Risk in a relationship develops through a pattern over time, not only through a single prohibited message, so safety cannot be a one-off filter bolted on at the door. Recent role-play research makes the same point: harms accumulate across a history, not within a single turn.
  • Measure growth beyond the product. Track whether people are becoming more capable outside it, and resist the temptation to read dependence as success.
  • Create graceful endings. Let people pause, leave, export what mattered or close a chapter without the experience behaving as though someone has died.
  • Protect children differently. Age-appropriate design, parental understanding, stronger privacy and stricter relational boundaries belong in the foundations, not taped on after launch. This is exactly the direction of UNICEF's 2026 work on AI companions and children, and of the new rules in some jurisdictions that now ban engineered dependence and virtual partners for minors outright.

What this means for education, entertainment and everyday products

These ideas land differently across the places I spend my working life, so it is worth being concrete.

  • Education. The most useful AI tutor is not an answer machine. It increases curiosity, independence and a learner's willingness to work with teachers and peers, and it knows the difference between helping someone think and thinking for them.
  • Media and entertainment. A character can absolutely live on beyond a film, a game or a story, and that continuity can deepen imagination. The failure mode is turning every fictional relationship into an endless subscription and calling that world-building.
  • Product experience. A conversational guide can quietly remove confusing menus and forms, which is a real gift. But the personality has to serve the person's goal rather than becoming ornamental theatre that gets in the way of it. The same instinct sits behind good training and onboarding experiences, where a human presence guiding someone through complexity beats a slicker interface that leaves them on their own.
  • Everyday technology. Relational behaviour is arriving in cars, homes, health tools, work platforms and personal devices whether teams plan for it or not. The decision worth making on purpose is what kind of relationship the product is inviting, before adding a friendly voice and hoping for the best.

A bridge should lead somewhere

The future of relational AI should not be judged by whether machines become convincing enough to replace our relationships. That is a low bar, and clearing it is not much of an achievement. It should be judged by whether the technology helps people create, learn, act, understand themselves better and move towards richer human lives.

Human attachment to these systems deserves to be taken seriously without any pretence that the system is human. Relational products carry heavier responsibilities than ordinary interfaces, and engagement becomes harmful the moment it starts rewarding dependency. The real opportunity is not to install a digital best friend into everything you make. It is to build purposeful, bounded relationships that genuinely help, and then have the confidence to let people walk away better than they arrived.

The best relational AI will not fight to become the most important character in your life. It will help you walk back into the story already happening around you.
